AUD Overview & Exam Format

⏱️ Time: 4 hours

📝 Format: 50 MCQs + 7 TBSs

📊 Weight: MCQs 50%, TBSs 50%

📈 Typical Pass Rate: ~48-53%

🧠 How AUD is Different

Unlike FAR (calculation-heavy), AUD is concept and judgment-heavy. The "right answer" often depends on professional skepticism, risk assessment, and understanding why an auditor does something — not just what they do.

Key Topics & Content Areas

Area I: Ethics, Professional Responsibilities & General Principles (15-25%)

  • AICPA Code of Professional Conduct — Independence, integrity, objectivity, due care
  • Independence Rules — Covered members, financial interests, non-audit services
  • Quality Control Standards (SQCS) — The 6 elements of quality control
  • Professional Skepticism — The mindset that underlies everything in audit

Area II: Assessing Risk & Developing a Planned Response (20-30%)

  • Audit Risk Model — AR = IR × CR × DR (the formula that drives everything)
  • Materiality — Planning materiality vs performance materiality vs posting materiality
  • Risk Assessment Procedures — Inquiries, analytics, observation, inspection
  • Fraud Risk — Fraud triangle (pressure, opportunity, rationalization), management override
  • Internal Control Frameworks — COSO Internal Control — Integrated Framework

Area III: Performing Further Procedures & Obtaining Evidence (30-40%)

  • Substantive Procedures — Tests of details, substantive analytical procedures
  • Tests of Controls — When required, how to design them
  • Sampling — Attribute sampling vs variable sampling, sample size factors
  • Specific Audit Areas — Cash, AR, inventory, AP, debt, equity, revenue
  • Using the Work of Others — Internal auditors, specialists, component auditors

Area IV: Forming Conclusions & Reporting (15-25%)

  • Audit Reports — Unmodified vs modified (qualified, adverse, disclaimer)
  • Emphasis-of-Matter & Other-Matter Paragraphs — When to use each
  • Group Audits — Component materiality, group engagement team responsibilities
  • Other Engagements — Reviews (SSARS), compilations, agreed-upon procedures (SSAE)

Audit Reports Cheat Sheet

Opinion TypeWhen UsedWording
Unmodified (Clean)FS fairly presented, no material issues"Present fairly, in all material respects…"
QualifiedMaterial but NOT pervasive misstatement or scope limitation"Except for… present fairly…"
AdverseMaterial AND pervasive misstatement"Do NOT present fairly…"
DisclaimerUnable to obtain sufficient evidence; material AND pervasive"We do NOT express an opinion…"

🎯 High-Yield: Material + Pervasive = Adverse/Disclaimer

The distinction between qualified and adverse/disclaimer comes down to pervasiveness. If the issue is material but limited to specific accounts/items = qualified. If it affects the financial statements as a whole = adverse or disclaimer.

Pro Tips for Passing AUD

  1. Think like an auditor. Every AUD question tests your ability to apply professional skepticism. Ask: "What would a careful, independent auditor do?"
  2. Master the Audit Risk Model. AR = IR × CR × DR. If you can manipulate this formula in any direction, you'll answer 10+ questions correctly.
  3. Know your reports cold. Unmodified vs Qualified vs Adverse vs Disclaimer — and exactly when each applies. This is guaranteed to show up.
  4. Understand "Material AND Pervasive." This phrase determines whether you go qualified or adverse/disclaimer.
  5. Don't confuse SSARS and SSAE. SSARS = preparation, compilation, review of non-public entities. SSAE = attestation engagements (examinations, reviews, agreed-upon procedures).
  6. Read questions carefully. AUD questions frequently use double negatives and subtle wording traps. Slow down and parse every word.

Common AUD Mistakes

❌ Mistake #1: Memorizing Without Understanding Judgment

AUD isn't about rules — it's about judgment. When two answers seem correct, ask which one better demonstrates professional skepticism or a conservative approach.

❌ Mistake #2: Confusing the Engagement Types

Audit (PCAOB/GAAS) ≠ Review (SSARS) ≠ Compilation (SSARS) ≠ Agreed-Upon Procedures (SSAE). Different standards, different levels of assurance, different reports.
